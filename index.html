How to Book

Step-by-step guide

Look at the events on this app. Each event shows which interpreter will be there.
Check the venues list. Some venues always have interpreters. Some need you to ask first.
Contact the venue. Use the details in the venues list. Many venues have SignVideo for BSL users.
Tell the venue:
— You need BSL or ISL
— The event name and date
— How many people in your group
— If you need companion tickets
Ask for an email to confirm your booking. Save it. Bring it with you on the day.
Arrive early. Find the BSL/ISL viewing area. If you have a problem, ask venue staff for help.
Venues — You can book many shows through the venue directly. They will send you an email to confirm the booking and may also send you e-tickets to download onto your phone to bring on the day.

Ticket Vendors — For some shows, tickets need to be purchased via ticket vendors. Check the access information for BSL/HOH tickets available, or contact the access email as instructed.

The venue will tell you where the BSL/ISL viewing area is located and any further instructions.
💡

Quick Tips

Make booking easier

The access team knows about BSL and ISL. Don't call the normal ticket number.
Ask for seats where you can see the interpreter clearly. Many venues have a BSL/ISL viewing area.
Ask the venue to send you an email to confirm your interpreter is booked. Save the email. Bring it to the event.
Give yourself time so you can find the interpreter area and get comfortable before the show starts.

Know Your Rights

Your legal protections

You have the right to enjoy live events.

The law protects you.

This information is general guidance only, not legal advice. Laws and procedures may change. For advice about your specific situation, contact the EASS (England, Scotland, Wales), the Equality Commission (Northern Ireland), or the IHREC (Ireland). Performance Interpreting Ltd is not a law firm and does not provide legal services.

The Law (UK) ▼

The Equality Act 2010 covers England, Scotland, and Wales. It says venues and event providers must make "reasonable adjustments" so Deaf people can access their services.

Interpreters are one example of a reasonable adjustment.

In Northern Ireland, similar protection comes from the Disability Discrimination Act 1995.

Your Rights in Ireland (ISL) ▼

Ireland has different laws from the UK.

The Irish Sign Language Act 2017 officially recognises ISL as a native language of the Deaf community. It gives Deaf people the right to use ISL when accessing public services and places a legal duty on public bodies to provide ISL interpreters.

Important: This duty mainly applies to public bodies, not automatically to private event organisers.

For cultural events (concerts, theatre, festivals), the ISL Act recognises that Deaf people should be able to access social, cultural and educational activities. Section 9 of the Act created a state-funded support scheme run by the Sign Language Interpreting Service (SLIS).

Through the SLIS Social Inclusion Voucher Scheme, Deaf ISL users can request interpreters for events. Sometimes the interpreter is funded by the scheme rather than the event organiser.

Learn more about SLIS

For events and venues, the Equal Status Acts 2000-2018 say venues must make "reasonable accommodation" for Deaf people, provided it does not involve more than a "nominal cost" to the venue.

If a venue in Ireland refuses, you can complain to the Workplace Relations Commission (WRC).

  • Notify the venue in writing using Form ES-1 within 2 months
  • File your complaint with the WRC within 6 months
  • It is free

Who can help in Ireland

  • Irish Deaf Society (IDS) - advocacy and advice
  • Sign Language Interpreting Service (SLIS) - interpreter guidance
  • Irish Human Rights and Equality Commission (IHREC) - rights guidance

Northern Ireland

Northern Ireland is not covered by the Equality Act 2010. Instead, the Disability Discrimination Act 1995 protects your right to access services, including events and venues.

Venues must make reasonable adjustments so Deaf people are not left out.

If a venue refuses, you can contact the Equality Commission for Northern Ireland (ECNI) for advice and support.

What Are Reasonable Adjustments for BSL Users? ▼

In the Equality Act, "reasonable adjustments" are deemed as auxiliary aids for Deaf people. This means the support required to make the show accessible.

Examples of auxiliary aids:

  • Interpreters
  • Captions
  • Lip Speakers
  • Loop Systems

The aid must be appropriate to your needs to access the event.

What counts as "reasonable" depends on things like the size of the event and the resources of the venue. Courts look at this on a case-by-case basis.

Planning Ahead ▼

Under the Equality Act 2010, venues have an "anticipatory duty". This means they must think about accessibility before anyone asks.

In practice, this means venues should:

  • Have an access policy
  • Put access information on their website
  • Have a process for booking interpreters when needed
  • Train staff so they know how to help

This does not mean every event must have an interpreter from the start. But venues should have a plan in place so they can respond when you ask.

You Don't Pay for Access ▼

You should not pay extra for access. The cost of an interpreter is for the venue or event organiser, not you.

The law says an event organiser cannot pass the cost of reasonable adjustments on to the person who needs them.

Companion / PA Tickets ▼

Many venues offer a free or discounted ticket for a companion or Personal Assistant (PA). Ask the access team when you book.

This is standard at most arenas, theatres, and festivals. Attitude Is Everything includes free companion/PA tickets as a Bronze-level requirement in their Live Events Access Charter - a framework that venues and festivals sign up to for improving accessibility.

If you need a companion to access the event, the venue should try to help. Ask them what they offer.

Nimbus Access Card ▼

Many venues now accept the Nimbus Access Card. This saves you having to provide evidence of your access needs every time you use a venue.

Some venues have their own access system, so please check with them.

Apply for a Nimbus Access Card

Common Questions About Access ▼

"The venue has a hearing loop. Is that enough?"
A hearing loop helps hearing aid users. A loop on its own does not provide access to communication for BSL/ISL users. The support provided must be appropriate to your needs to access the event, i.e. a BSL/ISL interpreter.

"The venue said nobody has asked before."
Under the anticipatory duty, venues should have a plan for access, even before someone asks. But you may still need to make a request for a specific event.

BSL Act 2022 ▼

The BSL Act 2022 says BSL is an official language of England, Wales, and Scotland.

The BSL Act does not give you new rights at events. It does not force venues to provide BSL.

Your main protection at events comes from the Equality Act 2010.

What To Do If a Venue Says No ▼

Step 1: Write to the venue. Ask for a "reasonable adjustment" under the Equality Act 2010 (for England, Wales and Scotland), or the Disability Discrimination Act 1995 (Northern Ireland). Keep a copy of your message.

Step 2: If the venue does not help, contact the Equality Advisory Support Service (EASS). They have BSL support.

  • Phone: 0808 800 0082
  • Textphone: 0808 800 0084
  • BSL support on their website
  • Open Mon-Fri 9am-7pm, Sat 10am-2pm

Step 3: If the venue still says no, you can consider taking legal action. You have 6 months.

You can also report access issues to Attitude Is Everything. They work with venues to improve access.
